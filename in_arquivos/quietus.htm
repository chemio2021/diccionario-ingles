Quietus (kwy-EE-tuhs) noun

 

   1. A final stroke that settles something.

   2. Discharge from life; death.

   3. A release from a duty or debt.

 

[Short for Middle English quietus est (he is quit), a formula of discharge

from a debt or other obligation, from Medieval Latin quietus est, from Latin,

quietus, past participle of quiescere (to rest), from quies (rest, quiet).]

 

The words acquit, quiet, quiescent, acquiesce, are some of the cousins of

today's word, all descendants of the same common root.

 

  "It is not so much that writing provided an escape from the perils of the

   Revolution, but rather that the Revolution afforded a distraction from

   his inward soul-wrestling and austere studies and perpetual susceptibility

   to the lure of an easeful quietus."

   Andy Martin, Napoleon on Happiness, Raritan (New Brunswick, New Jersey),

   Spring 2000.

 

  "The irony is, of course, that the Curse will provide its own quietus and

   lead me to the peace I have so desperately sought."

   Michael Bywater, Not With a Whimper But With a Bang, Independent on Sunday

   (London, UK), May 19, 1996.
