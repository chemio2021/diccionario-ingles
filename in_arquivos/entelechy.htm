Entelechy (en-TEL-uh-kee) noun

 

   1. Perfect realization as opposed to a potentiality.

   2. In some philosophies, a vital force that propels one to self-fulfillment.

 

[From Late Latin entelechia, from Greek entelecheia, from enteles (complete), from telos (end, completion) + echein (to have).]

 

  "It concerns our final end, our entelechy, the purpose of our existence,

   where we are going to go."

   Gray Henry; The First Prophet; Parabola (New York); Spring 1996.

 

  "As movies directed by ex-Star Trek actors go, it isn't nearly as jejune

   as, say, Leonard Nimoy's Three Men and a Baby, but neither does it

   possess the ambivalent entelechy of LeVar Burton's The Tiger Woods Story."

   Michael Atkinson; Three Woman and an Organ; The Village Voice

   (New York); Apr 9, 2002.
